Algebraic Fractions





Simplifying an algebraic fraction consists of 2 steps:
  1. Factorising.
  2. Cancelling.

I've made this example, to make it super clear:

It's very straightforward. You factorise both sides of the fraction, and then if there are any parts in common between the sides, you can cancel them down.
Remember that you cannot cancel down terms which are connected to other parts of the fraction with + or -, only with multiplication connections.



To add and subtract any fraction, you need to make the denominator the same.
This is the same for algebraic fractions.
Let's take a/x + b/y.

  1. To make the common denominator xy, you need to multiply the numerators by the opposite denominator. Remember, whatever you do to the bottom, you have to do to the top.
  2. So, for this example, we'll multiply a/x by y, and b/y by x to make ay/xy + bx/xy.
  3. Now it's easy to add them together because we can simply add the numerators together. The answer is ay+bx/xy.



Like all fractions, dividing algebraic fractions is the same method as multiplying them, but you flip the second fraction and change the division symbol into a multiply symbol.

  1. Factorise parts of your fractions if you need to.
  2. Cancel down and/or diagonally.
  3. Multiply what's left.
  4. Simplify as much as possible at the end.
